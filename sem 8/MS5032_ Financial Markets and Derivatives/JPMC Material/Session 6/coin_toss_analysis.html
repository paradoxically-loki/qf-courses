Step 1: Bernoulli Distribution — A Single Coin Toss

A single coin toss is the simplest random experiment. It's called a Bernoulli trial — exactly two outcomes with fixed probabilities.

Bernoulli Distribution:
\[ X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p), \quad X = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{(Heads), probability } p \\ 0 & \text{(Tails), probability } 1-p \end{cases} \]

For a fair coin, \( p = 0.5 \). The key statistics are:

Mean (Expected Value): \( \mu = E[X] = p = 0.5 \)

Variance: \( \sigma^2 = \text{Var}(X) = p(1-p) = 0.25 \)

Standard Deviation: \( \sigma = \sqrt{p(1-p)} = 0.5 \)

Trader's Perspective

From the trader's view, each toss yields \( X_i \in \{-1, +1\} \):

\[ X_i = \begin{cases} -1 & \text{Heads (pays \$1)} \\ +1 & \text{Tails (receives \$1)} \end{cases} \] \[ E[X_i] = 0, \quad \text{Var}(X_i) = 1 \]
Key Insight: A single toss is the atomic building block. It has zero expected value (fair game) but non-zero variance (uncertainty exists). This is our starting point.

Step 2: Binomial Distribution — Counting Heads in n Tosses

Toss the coin \( n \) times independently and count the total number of heads:

Sum of Bernoulli trials:
\[ S_n = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n, \quad S_n \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p) \]
Probability Mass Function:
\[ P(S_n = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \]
Mean and Variance:
\[ \mu = np, \quad \sigma^2 = np(1-p), \quad \sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)} \]

Step 3: The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The CLT is one of the most important results in probability theory. It explains why the Binomial converges to the Normal.

Central Limit Theorem — Formal Statement:

Let \( X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n \) be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables with mean \( \mu \) and finite variance \( \sigma^2 \). Then the standardised sum converges in distribution to a standard Normal: \[ Z_n = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n X_i - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty \]

What This Means in Plain English

No matter what the original distribution of each \( X_i \) looks like (coin toss, dice roll, stock return, etc.), if you add up enough independent copies, the sum will look like a bell curve.

Applied to Our Coin Toss

For \( S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \) where \( X_i \in \{-1, +1\} \):

\[ E[X_i] = 0, \quad \text{Var}(X_i) = 1 \] \[ E[S_n] = 0, \quad \text{Var}(S_n) = n, \quad \sigma_{S_n} = \sqrt{n} \]
By CLT, for large n:
\[ S_n \approx \mathcal{N}(0, n) \]

The Three Key Properties of CLT

PropertyDescriptionFormula
UniversalityWorks regardless of original distribution shapeOnly needs finite mean and variance
Rate of ConvergenceBerry-Esseen: error bounded by \( O(1/\sqrt{n}) \)\( |F_n(x) - \Phi(x)| \leq \frac{C \cdot E|X|^3}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}} \)

The Normal Distribution — The Limit

Probability Density Function:
\[ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \]
Standard Normal (\( \mu=0, \sigma=1 \)):
\[ \phi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2} \]
Key Quantiles:
\[ P(Z < -2.3263) = 0.01 \quad \text{(1st percentile)} \] \[ P(Z < -1.6449) = 0.05 \quad \text{(5th percentile)} \] \[ P(Z < 0) = 0.50 \quad \text{(median)} \]

Why CLT Matters for Risk Management

Once CLT applies, we only need two numbers — the mean and the variance — to fully characterise the distribution of the sum. This is why:
  • VaR can be computed analytically (no simulation needed for linear portfolios)
  • Risk scales as \( \sqrt{n} \), not \( n \)
  • Diversification works — independent risks partially cancel

Rule of Thumb: When Is Normal Approximation OK?

For Binomial \( \to \) Normal: The approximation is adequate when: \[ np \geq 5 \quad \text{AND} \quad n(1-p) \geq 5 \] For a fair coin (\( p = 0.5 \)), this means \( n \geq 10 \).

With our 1,000 tosses: \( np = 500 \gg 5 \). The Normal approximation is excellent.

Step 4: The Entry Deposit Problem — 1 Player, 1000 Tosses

The trader's net P&L after 1,000 tosses (before entry fee):

\[ S_{1000} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1000) \] \[ \mu = 0, \quad \sigma = \sqrt{1000} \approx 31.62 \]

The Trader's Requirement

Let \( F \) = entry deposit. Total profit = \( F + S \). The trader wants:

\[ P(F + S \geq 0) \geq 0.99 \] \[ \Leftrightarrow P(S < -F) \leq 0.01 \]

Solving for F

Standardising:
\[ P\left(Z < \frac{-F}{\sqrt{1000}}\right) = 0.01 \] \[ \frac{-F}{\sqrt{1000}} = z_{0.01} = -2.3263 \] \[ \boxed{F = 2.3263 \times \sqrt{1000} \approx \$73.57} \]
Interpretation: The trader takes an entry deposit equal to ~2.33 standard deviations of downside risk — essentially the Value-at-Risk (VaR) at the 99% confidence level. After the game, net losses are deducted and the remaining deposit is returned to the player.

Step 5: 100 Players — The Power of Diversification

Now the trader offers the game to 100 players simultaneously. Each plays 1,000 tosses independently.

Each player's game: \( S_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1000) \), \( j = 1, \ldots, 100 \)

Total trader P&L: \( T = \sum_{j=1}^{100} S_j \)

Since games are independent: \[ E[T] = 0, \quad \text{Var}(T) = 100 \times 1000 = 100{,}000 \] \[ \sigma_T = \sqrt{100{,}000} \approx 316.23 \]

Solving for Per-Person Entry Deposit

Total fees = \( 100F \). Require \( P(100F + T \geq 0) \geq 0.99 \): \[ 100F = 2.3263 \times \sqrt{100{,}000} \] \[ 100F = 735.70 \] \[ \boxed{F = \$7.36 \text{ per person}} \]
The deposit dropped from $73.57 to $7.36 — a 10x reduction!
The factor is exactly \( 1/\sqrt{n_{\text{players}}} = 1/\sqrt{100} = 1/10 \).

The Diversification Formula

General per-person deposit: \[ F_{\text{per person}} = \frac{z_{\alpha} \cdot \sigma_{\text{single game}}}{\sqrt{n_{\text{players}}}} = \frac{z_{\alpha} \cdot \sqrt{n_{\text{tosses}}}}{\sqrt{n_{\text{players}}}} \]
Key scaling law: \[ F \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{n_{\text{players}}}} \]

Step 6: The Critical Comparison — Same Tosses, Different Structure

Both strategies involve 100,000 total coin tosses. But the structure is fundamentally different:

Strategy A: 1 Player x 100,000 Tosses

  • Entry deposit: $735.70 (one person)
  • Single point of failure
  • One counterparty bears all risk
  • No diversification across players
  • If player defaults: total loss
  • Hard to find customers at $735 deposit

Strategy B: 100 Players x 1,000 Tosses

  • Entry deposit: $7.36 per person
  • Risk spread across 100 counterparties
  • Losses from one offset by gains from others
  • Full diversification benefit
  • If one defaults: only 1% deposit loss
  • Accessible price point, large market

Why the Total Deposit Is the Same

Both strategies have identical aggregate risk:
\[ \sigma_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{100{,}000} \approx 316.23 \quad \text{(same in both cases)} \] \[ \text{Total deposit} = 2.3263 \times 316.23 = \$735.70 \quad \text{(same in both cases)} \]

The math is identical because the trader's aggregate P&L follows the same distribution:

\[ T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 100{,}000) \quad \text{in both scenarios} \]

The Trap: Per-Game vs Aggregate Protection

ApproachLogicTotal DepositEfficiency
Per-game 99% CIEach game protected individually$7,3571x (wasteful)
Aggregate 99% CIPortfolio-level protection$735.7010x more efficient
The diversification benefit ratio: \[ \frac{\text{Per-game deposits}}{\text{Aggregate deposits}} = \frac{n \times z\sigma_1}{z\sigma_1\sqrt{n}} = \sqrt{n} = \sqrt{100} = 10 \]
Warning: Summing individual VaRs overstates portfolio risk by a factor of \( \sqrt{n} \). This is why risk is always computed at the portfolio level, not by adding position-level VaRs.
